### Story 1: Leadership / Initiative — Open Source → Linux Foundation Energy

**Headline:** I took Hydro-Quebec from zero open-source culture to being the first North American utility to enter the Linux Foundation Energy.

**Situation:**
- When I joined Hydro-Quebec, open-source development was essentially non-existent.
- No culture for it, no process, no precedent.

**Task:**
- I saw the value — for the organization, for the work, for the field — and I decided to push for it.
- Nobody asked me to. I just believed it was the right direction.

**Action:**
- Talked to my manager, explained the value of open source.
- Presented to my colleagues, to the director of the research center, and to the director of the IT department.
- Went to a Linux Foundation conference and presented my work on a cloud-edge platform to coordinate distributed energy resources.
- The manager came back convinced. He became an ally — a key one.

**Result:**
- Hydro-Quebec entered the Linux Foundation Energy.
- First utility in North America to do so.
- The manager who pushed internally got most of the credit — and only a handful of people know I was pushing from the beginning.
- But that's fine. As my boss always says: it's not about who is right, but what is right.

### Story 2: Failure / Resilience — 3-Year Project Closed → Repackaged → HEMS Foundation

*Juan already structured this one in Headline/Situation/Action/Outcome form — keeping his structure, relabeling Outcome as Result for consistency.*

**Headline:** I worked hard for over 3 years on a project I loved — it was closed without real-world impact. What came next is what matters.

**Situation:**
- I worked on a project I loved. Very complex: orchestrating distributed energy resources with price signals and market structures, cloud-edge, IoT, machine learning — the dream project.
- I took it personally. I worked very hard. Developed almost everything from scratch.
- After 3 years, the project was closed.
- I was not the leader, not the Project Manager — but it still felt like a failure to me.
- The reason it felt like failure: all my work would be wasted.

**Action:**
- I took parts of the code and repackaged them into smaller blocks of functionality.
- Those smaller blocks were transferred to other projects.
- Eventually, they were published as open-source projects on Hydro-Quebec's official open-source repository.

**Result:**
- The packages I created are still used on other projects today.
- And now they are the mathematical foundation for the internal HEMS (Home Energy Management System) being built internally — something that will actually be used.
- The work wasn't wasted. It just took a different path to get there.
