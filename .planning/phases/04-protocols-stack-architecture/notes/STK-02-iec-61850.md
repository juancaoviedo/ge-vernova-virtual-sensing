## 1. Three-Tier Hierarchy — Process / Bay / Station (Deep)

The IEC 61850 standard organizes a substation into three tiers. This is the **substation**
hierarchy — not the field/edge/fog/cloud architecture from STK-05.

| Tier | Layer | What Lives Here | Primary IEC 61850 Services |
|------|-------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Process level** | Bottom — the switchyard | Circuit breakers, current/voltage transformers (CTs/VTs), merging units, actuators | **GOOSE** (protection trips, interlocking signals) + **SV** (digitized I/V samples from merging units) |
| **Bay level** | Middle — the relay room | IEDs: protection relays, bay controllers | Receives GOOSE + SV from process; sends **MMS** up to station level |
| **Station level** | Top — the control room | SCADA HMI, engineering workstation, gateway/RTU to control center | **MMS** (client-server supervision, setpoints, reports to/from control center) |

**Interview one-liner (say verbatim):**
"Process level is the switchyard — GOOSE and SV never leave the LAN. Bay level is the
protection relay and bay controller that act on them. Station level is the SCADA HMI that
talks MMS over TCP to the control center."

---

## 2. GOOSE / SV / MMS Roles (Deep)

The three services of IEC 61850 each exist at a different layer and serve a fundamentally
different purpose. The distinction that interviewers probe is **Layer-2 vs. routable**:

| Service | Full Name | Layer | Latency | Direction | Use Case |
|---------|-----------|-------|---------|-----------|----------|
| **GOOSE** | Generic Object Oriented Substation Event | Ethernet Layer 2 — **no IP** | **< 4 ms** | Publisher → multicast subscribers | Protection trips, interlock signals, breaker status changes |
| **SV** | Sampled Values | Ethernet Layer 2 — **no IP** | Sub-ms, high rate | Merging unit → IED | Digitized current + voltage waveforms on the process bus |
| **MMS** | Manufacturing Message Specification (ISO 9506) | TCP/IP | Seconds (supervisory) | Client (SCADA) ↔ Server (IED) | Reading measurements, writing setpoints, retrieving event logs |

**Key structural fact — say this exactly:**
"GOOSE and SV are Layer 2 — they never leave the substation LAN and are never routed. MMS
is TCP/IP and is routable, so that's the path from substation to control center."

**GOOSE retransmission mechanism (why it achieves <4 ms reliably):**
On a state change, GOOSE retransmits at approximately 2 ms intervals, then backs off to a
slow heartbeat. Reliability comes from **repetition, not acknowledgment** — this is what
makes the latency deterministic. There is no TCP handshake, no IP routing decision; a
multicast Ethernet frame traverses the substation LAN switch in microseconds.

## 3. Logical Nodes — The Criterion-2 Gap (Deep)

This is the section CLAUDE.md does not cover and that you cannot fake. Every IEC 61850
logical node class name is **exactly four letters**. The **first letter** indicates the
functional group. Know the five nodes below; be ready to name at least three fluently.

| LN Name | Group | Full Name / Function | Interview One-Liner |
|---------|-------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **XCBR** | X — Switchgear | Circuit Breaker | "The circuit breaker itself — carries Pos (position) and OpCnt (operation count); the thing CSWI commands" |
| **MMXU** | M — Measurement | Three-Phase Measurement Unit | "Real-time V, I, power, power factor, frequency — the most-used measurement LN in any IED" |
| **CSWI** | C — Control | Switch Controller | "The operator interface: open/close from SCADA hits CSWI, which verifies interlocking via CILO, then commands XCBR" |
| **PTOC** | P — Protection | Time Overcurrent Protection | "Overcurrent protection (IEEE device 50/51) — Op is the trip output, Str is the start signal" |
| **PDIS** | P — Protection | Distance Protection | "Impedance-based distance protection (IEEE device 21) — triggers when fault impedance falls inside a protection zone" |

**Four-letter rule:** first letter = functional group. Examples:
- X = Switchgear (XCBR, XSWI)
- M = Measurement (MMXU, MMTR)
- C = Control (CSWI, CILO — CILO is the interlock LN)
- P = Protection (PTOC, PDIS, PSCH, PDIF, many more)

**Bonus nodes from the IEC TR 61850-10-3:2022 source PDF (useful for credibility):**

| LN Name | Group | Function |
|---------|-------|----------|
| **LLN0** | L — System | Root LN of every Logical Device; hosts GOOSE control blocks, datasets, and report control blocks |
| **LPHD** | L — System | Physical device health and nameplate; the Sim.stVal attribute here enables test mode |
| **TCTR** | T — Instrument Transformer | Current transformer model — used with merging units on the process bus |
| **TVTR** | T — Instrument Transformer | Voltage transformer model |

**The 13 LN functional groups (awareness — name the letters, not every node):**
L (System), P (Protection), X (Switchgear), M (Measurement), C (Control),
R (Protection-Related: RREC=recloser, RBRF=breaker failure), A (Automatic Control),
S (Sensor/Monitoring), T (Instrument Transformer), Y (Power Transformer),
Z (Further Equipment: ZGEN=generator), I (Interfacing), G (Generic: GGIO)

## 4. The Command Flow — Ties It Together (Deep)

This is the rehearsal sequence that connects the logical nodes to the services and the tiers.
Say it aloud until it flows:

**"Dispatcher issues 'open breaker' → hits CSWI (the controller LN) → CSWI verifies
interlocking via CILO → sends operate command to XCBR (the circuit breaker LN) → XCBR
changes Pos and emits a GOOSE message → other IEDs receive the GOOSE in <4 ms."**

This one sentence demonstrates: you know CSWI is the control LN, XCBR is the equipment LN,
CILO is the interlock check, and GOOSE is the Layer-2 mechanism that broadcasts the state
change — all without notes.

---

## 5. Pitfall Guard — Which Hierarchy Are You Describing? (Awareness)

When you speak about "tiers" in this interview, you may be asked about two different
hierarchies:

- **IEC 61850 substation hierarchy:** process / bay / station — describes what lives
  **inside a single substation**. GOOSE and SV live at the process level; MMS bridges bay
  to station and station to the wider control center network.

- **STK-05 four-tier reference architecture:** field / edge / fog / cloud — describes the
  **broader virtual-sensing system** from switchyard sensors up to cloud historian and model
  training. The field tier maps roughly to IEC 61850's process + bay levels; the edge tier
  includes the K3s node running the EKF inference above the bay level.

**When speaking: label which hierarchy you mean.** Say "within the substation, the process
level..." or "in the broader four-tier architecture, the edge tier...". Confusing the two
is Pitfall 6 (from the research notes) and is a clear signal that you learned IEC 61850
as a phrase without understanding the architecture.

## <3-min say-aloud version

> "IEC 61850 is the standard that organizes substation automation into three tiers: process,
> bay, and station.
>
> The **process level** is the switchyard: circuit breakers, current and voltage transformers,
> merging units. Two IEC 61850 services live here — **GOOSE** and **Sampled Values**. GOOSE
> is a multicast Ethernet Layer-2 message — no IP — that carries a protection trip or interlock
> signal in **less than 4 milliseconds**. It retransmits at ~2 ms intervals on state change;
> reliability comes from repetition, not acknowledgment. Sampled Values carry the digitized
> current and voltage waveforms from the merging unit to the protection relay — also Layer-2.
>
> The **bay level** is the relay room: protection relays and bay controllers that receive GOOSE
> and SV from below and talk MMS upward. **MMS** — Manufacturing Message Specification — is
> TCP/IP and is routable. It's the supervisory path: SCADA reads measurements, writes setpoints,
> retrieves event logs. Seconds-class latency, not milliseconds.
>
> The **station level** is the SCADA HMI and the gateway to the control center.
>
> Now the logical nodes. Every IEC 61850 device function is modeled as a named object. The names
> are four letters, first letter = functional group. Five I can name cold:
> - **XCBR** — the circuit breaker itself (X=Switchgear); carries position Pos and OpCnt.
> - **MMXU** — three-phase measurement unit (M=Measurement); V, I, power, frequency.
> - **CSWI** — switch controller (C=Control); the open/close operator interface.
> - **PTOC** — time overcurrent protection (P=Protection); IEEE device 50/51.
> - **PDIS** — distance protection (P=Protection); IEEE device 21, fault impedance zone.
>
> The command flow: dispatcher issues 'open breaker' → CSWI (controller) checks interlocking
> via CILO → commands XCBR → XCBR changes Pos → emits a GOOSE multicast → other IEDs get it
> in <4 ms. That's the whole loop."

## → Bridge to your work

> **"I know publish/subscribe — that instinct maps directly to GOOSE's publisher/multicast
> model. The architectural difference is that GOOSE pushes that pattern down to Layer-2
> deterministic protection where <4 ms is a hard guarantee, not a QoS level. MMS is the
> routable, TCP/IP supervisory channel — that's where I'd integrate at the station level,
> and it looks more like the MQTT broker pattern I know."**

| IEC 61850 service | Juan's closest analog | What's different |
|-------------------|-----------------------|-----------------|
| **GOOSE** (Layer-2 multicast, <4 ms protection) | MQTT pub/sub (application tier) | GOOSE is deterministic Layer-2; MQTT is application-tier TCP; not routable vs. routable |
| **SV** (digitized I/V stream from merging unit) | InfluxDB time-series ingest | SV is sub-ms real-time on a process bus; InfluxDB is a push-based store with seconds latency |
| **MMS** (TCP/IP client-server supervision) | MQTT + FastAPI REST (OSED SCADA-equivalent) | MMS is a standardized ISO 9506 protocol; same client-server pattern as what I've built |

**How to say this in the interview:**

> "My pub/sub instinct from OSED maps to GOOSE's publish/multicast model — but GOOSE is
> that pattern pushed to <4 ms Layer-2 determinism for protection, where you cannot afford
> an IP routing hop or a TCP handshake. MMS is the routable SCADA channel I'd integrate at
> the station level, and it looks very much like the MQTT-to-FastAPI supervisory layer I've
> already built. So IEC 61850 is not alien to me — it's the same architectural instincts
> with much harder real-time constraints at the process level."

## Quick-Recall Card (Recite Before the Interview)

1. **Three substation tiers (IEC 61850, NOT STK-05):** Process (switchyard) → Bay (relay room) → Station (SCADA/HMI).
2. **GOOSE:** Layer-2 Ethernet — no IP; **<4 ms**; multicast; protection trips + interlocks; retransmits at ~2 ms, reliability by repetition.
3. **SV (Sampled Values):** Layer-2 Ethernet — no IP; sub-ms; merging unit → IED; digitized I/V waveforms on process bus.
4. **MMS:** TCP/IP (ISO 9506); seconds; SCADA client ↔ IED server; read/write setpoints, retrieve logs; **routable** — this is what goes WAN to control center.
5. **Layer-2 vs. routable:** "GOOSE and SV never leave the substation LAN; MMS is TCP/IP — that's the WAN path."
6. **XCBR** — circuit breaker (X=Switchgear); Pos + OpCnt.
7. **MMXU** — three-phase measurement (M=Measurement); V/I/P/PF/freq — most-used LN.
8. **CSWI** — switch controller (C=Control); open/close from SCADA, interlocking via CILO.
9. **PTOC** — time overcurrent protection (P=Protection); IEEE 50/51; Op=trip, Str=start.
10. **PDIS** — distance protection (P=Protection); IEEE 21; fault impedance zone trigger.
11. **Command flow:** CSWI (verify interlock via CILO) → XCBR (Pos change) → GOOSE multicast → other IEDs in <4 ms.
12. **Pitfall guard:** IEC 61850 three tiers = inside the substation. STK-05 four tiers = the broader field/edge/fog/cloud system. Label which one you mean.
